Overview

The Game

Realms of Legacy is an asymmetrical roleplaying game where you step

into the shoes of a Veildiver, a cosmic sailor, joining a crew to carve

out a legacy across space. Fight with sword and sorcery, survey

uncharted lands, build relations with otherworldly creatures, and engage

in magitech ship warfare. You decide how to build your legacy throughout

the realms, whether you work for a great Guild Company or set off on

your own.

Important Terminology

There are some common terms that you should be aware of going into this

game:

The Veil: What we refer to as outer space or the cosmos is what the

creatures of this game refer to as the Veil. The unknown region beyond

the boundaries of the solar system is known as the Outerveil.

The Shroud: A plane of ethereal existence that mirrors the Veil, the

Shroud is a realm where time flows at a fraction of its normal pace.

When mortals perish, their souls drift into this shadowy expanse,

wandering aimlessly, finding refuge in a mirrored realm, or falling prey

to powerful entities that can move freely between the Veil and the

Shroud.

Veilstriders: Sea-faring ships that have been upgraded to fly

through the cosmos of the Veil using their magitech Core engines and

solar sails. Their crews are referred to as Veildivers.

Plutheryte: A rare and ancient ore that holds pure magical energy

and is highly sought after. Its origins are unknown even to the oldest

entities within the Veil.

Sorce: The life of the Veil and source of all magic. Powerful

creatures can manipulate Sorce to cast Spells or enhance their martial

prowess.

Aspects: Often revered as gods of the Veil. There are six kinds of

Aspects known throughout the Veil, some controlling certain domains,

ideals, or vessels: Stellar (stars), Eternal (Black Holes),

Primal (Planets), Lunar (Moons), Cosmic (natural forces),

and Ascended (demigods).

\

Shadow Gloom is magical darkness that consumes all natural light.

Divine Light does not destroy it but does suppress it. Creatures with

Nightvision cannot see through Shadow Gloom.

Players

Players are the people who are playing the game. One person in the group

is the Game Master (GM), and the rest are Player Characters

(PCs).

Player Characters

You make a character as a PC using the rules in this book and your own

imagination. You can choose what your character does, thinks, and feels,

but only within the limits set by the game\'s rules and the GM\'s

guidance.

As a PC, you are encouraged to:

  • Embrace the game\'s premise and immerse yourself in its world.
  • Actively look for adventures, quests, and chances to improve your

legacy.

  • Work together with other PCs to create a friendly and helpful

atmosphere.

Game Master

You are in charge of making the world, its people, and the things that

the PCs interact with as the GM. Non-Player Characters (NPCs) are

the characters you make and control. Your main goal is to make sure that

everyone has a good time, not to beat the PCs. As the GM, you also

uphold the game\'s rules, providing clarification and making rulings as

necessary.

As a GM, you are encouraged to:

  • Offer a variety of scenarios and opportunities for adventure.
  • Give the PCs challenges that will test them but not overwhelm them.
  • Make quick and fair decisions when the rules are unclear.
  • Keep the story balanced by making sure that all the PCs get their

time in the spotlight.

Playing the Game

In Realms of Legacy, players can choose from three different

activities, all of which involve rolling a set of dice. You usually have

to roll a 6 or higher on at least one die to win these games.

Freeform Roleplay

This is the activity that players will take part in when not engaged in

any other activity. Time progresses fluidly, dictated by the players\'

actions and intentions. Whether resolving a conflict, engaging in

dialogue, or exploring a scene, this mode emphasizes character-driven

storytelling and environmental interaction.

Tactical Combat

In this intense activity, players find themselves in dangerous, violent

situations where they must work together and think strategically to

prevail. Time is broken down into 6-second intervals, emphasizing how

important each move and choice is. Success hinges on careful planning

and the ability to work as a cohesive unit.

Veilstrider Conflict

In this activity, players control huge magical ships called Veilstriders

and fight against enemy ships or Leviathans in high-stakes battles.

Everyone must adapt and cooperate as they all take on roles to manage

their vessel.

What You Need to Play

  • Two to six players with one elected as the GM.
  • These rules for reference in a format of your choice.
  • A handful of 6-sided and 8-sided dice (at least 6 of each).
  • Character sheets for each player playing as a PC and a means for

them to take notes.

  • A notebook or other means for a GM to take notes .

While not needed, it is highly recommended to have a calculator at hand.

How to Play

The Dice

Realms of Legacy is a dice pool game where players assume the roles of

various creatures adventuring together. You will roll a combination of

6-sided dice and 8-sided dice to perform tasks. This book

references these dice with a number on the left, the letter d, and a

number on the right. The number on the left represents the amount of

dice you need, and the number on the right represents the type of dice

by stating the number of sides those dice have. So, if the game calls

for 2d6, you roll two 6-sided dice. We recommend playing with 6d6 and

6d8. Fewer dice may be used, but players may need to record or remember

the numbers rolled on a die before rolling it again.

A d6 is a Base die, and a d8 is a Proficiency die. The more

Proficiency dice a character has when performing a task, the better they

perform it. Rolling one Proficiency die is more effective than rolling

two Base dice.

Checks

Checks are a mechanism used to determine the success or failure of an

action. When a player makes a check, they roll a pool of dice to

determine what happens. Each die that reads 6 or greater counts as one

success. Multiple successes on a single check can provide additional

benefits.

Skill checks are a core part of Realms of Legacy. Whenever a

Player Character (PC) or Non-Player Character (NPC) wants to perform an

action with an uncertain outcome, a Skill check will need to be

performed. In Skill checks, players create and roll a pool of dice (also

known as a dice pool). The number of dice rolled is based on the

Ranks of the Skill and Attribute required to execute the check.

To succeed on a Skill check, you must roll at least one success (6 or

higher on a single die).

Attributes and Skills

Each creature has four core Attributes, and each Attribute has four

associated Skills:

  • Strength: Brawl, Might, Endure, and Intimidate
  • Agility: Move, Shoot, Finesse, and Hide
  • Wits: Analyze, Scout, Insight, and Survival
  • Empathy: Manipulate, Medical, Perform, and Tame

Some abilities and traits allow players to make a Skill check

with an Attribute that is not associated with that Skill. Otherwise,

when making a Skill check, players must use the associated Attribute.

For further details on what each Attribute and Skill represents, refer

to Attributes and Skills Defined ([page

34](#_Attributes_and_Skills)).

Dice Pool Modifiers

Positive modifiers, represented by a (+) next to a number, add Base

dice to the dice pool. The number of Base dice added is indicated by the

number next to the (+). Some abilities and actions grant special

positive modifiers that provide Proficiency dice instead of Base dice.

You can't have more than six positive modifiers at once (+6).

Negative modifiers, represented by a (-) next to a number, remove

dice from the dice pool. A single negative modifier can take away any

die, including Proficiency dice. The player chooses which to remove, but

they must remove an amount equal to the negative modifier.

If the negative modifier would fully deplete a player's dice pool, they

must use only one Base die to attempt the check. You can't have more

than six negative modifiers at once (-6).

When multiple modifiers from different sources are in play, apply the

positive modifiers before the negative modifiers.

For common success rates of different dice pools, see the Chance of Success table on page 246, which lists the probabilities for commonly used dice pools.

Skill Check Step-by-Step

To understand how to construct a dice pool for a Skill check, follow

these guidelines:

CHECK YOUR STATS: First, determine what Skill applies and its

associated Attribute. Then, check the Skill's Rank and the Attribute's

Rank.

1) Which Rank has the highest value? Add that many Base dice to the

dice pool.

a. If your Brawl is Rank 2 and your Strength is Rank 3, add three

Base dice to your dice pool.

b. If the Skill and Attribute Ranks are both zero, you must use

only one Base die for the check.

2) Which Rank has the lowest value? Transform that many Base dice

into Proficiency dice.

If the Skill and Attribute Ranks are equal and greater than zero, then you get an amount of Proficiency Dice equal to the Rank of either one.

3) APPLY MODIFIER(S): After determining your dice pool for a check, apply any positive modifiers first by adding the indicated number of Base dice to your dice pool. Then address the negative modifiers by removing the indicated number of dice (starting with the Base dice and then Proficiency) from your dice pool.

Fabrizio's dice pool starts with three Base dice (from his Rank 3

Strength). He has Rank 2 Brawl, allowing him to transform two Base dice

into Proficiency dice. He now has a pool of 1d6 and 2d8.

Degrees of Success

Rolling at least one 6 or higher counts as a successful Skill check, but

achieving more than one success in a single roll can further enhance the

check's outcome. There are three degrees of success that can be applied

to some actions. Actions that do not specify any additional effects from

the degrees of success do not grant additional benefits.

  • Success: Rolling at least one success means that you succeed in

the task you are trying to accomplish.

  • Critical Success: Rolling three to four successes turns your

success into a critical success.

  • Supercritical Success: Rolling five or more successes turns your

success into a supercritical success.

Static vs Dynamic Checks

In Realms of Legacy, there are two types of Skill checks the GM can

call for:

Static checks are used when a creature wishes to execute an action

that is outlined in the rules.

  • The check required for any action is noted in the action's

description, as are the consequences for failing the check.

  • For actions that do not specify failure consequences, a failed

Static check indicates the action was unsuccessful.

  • Unless otherwise noted, a creature may try the action again if

circumstances permit, though this may consume additional time.

\

Dynamic checks are used when a creature wishes to undertake an action

that isn't explicitly outlined in these rules or when a creature aims to

use a defined action in an unanticipated manner. In this check, the GM

determines the relevant Skill and the two primary components of a

Dynamic Skill check: Difficulty and Danger.

Difficulty

This illustrates how challenging the action will be, given the

circumstances. The GM assigns one of four Difficulties: Moderate, Hard,

Extreme, or Nigh Impossible. If an action isn't at least Moderately

difficult, the GM should simply allow it with no roll.

To assess the Difficulty, GMs should consider both physical and social

obstacles present. Physical obstacles might include terrain,

structures, environmental conditions, or other barriers hindering the

PC. Social obstacles are the consequences for NPCs of being swayed

by the PCs. When assessing these obstacles, prioritize the most

significant challenges to determine the Difficulty.

Fabrizio tries to convince the guards that he is a new guard and needs access to the barracks. The guards aren't sure if he's telling the truth, and granting access to an imposter would have serious consequences since the Duke in charge has executed other guards for less. This is considered an Extreme social obstacle.

Danger

This illustrates the severity of consequences that can happen for

failing the check. Dynamic Skill checks have one of four Danger

categories: Safe, Risky, Dangerous, or Destructive.

The GM should always inform the players of the Danger of a Dynamic check even if their PCs aren't yet aware of the risk. This gives the players agency when crafting the narrative with the GM.

Safe: A failed check results in the inability to try that action again under the exact same circumstances. If conditions change or a new factor emerges, the GM might permit another attempt.

Risky: A failed check results in consequences that could include taking damage, losing a basic item, offending someone, or the emergence of a new complication in the scenario.

Dangerous: A failed check carries significant consequences that could include acquiring a Wound or Trauma, a piece of gear getting broken, starting a fight, or the emergence of a situation that jeopardizes the entire group.

Destructive: A failed check can result in a permanent Wound or Trauma, character death, or destruction of your reputation. This should be reserved for extreme circumstances or a situation that has escalated from the previous Danger.

Wounds and Traumas are physical and mental scars, detailed on [page

12](#impairment-and-injuries).

When a player fails a Dynamic Skill check, the GM can inflict one

consequence appropriate to that Danger Level. Although GMs are required

to disclose the Danger of the check before the player rolls, they don't

have to disclose the exact consequences they have chosen to inflict if

the check fails.

If a failed check would inflict damage to a PC, that PC may activate any defensive abilities against the damage.

Invoking Enhancements

When PCs make Dynamic checks, they can invoke up to three different

elements to affect their check. This can include traits, upbringings,

culture, active Spells, or even experiences that the PC has had. The

player must explain how the invoked element would help them in the

check. If the GM agrees the invoked elements are relevant and would

benefit the check, the player receives the benefit of that trait,

ability, or action on the Skill check.

If the invoked element doesn't provide a direct, written, mechanical

benefit tied to the Skill check, like an upbringing, the GM can bestow a

custom benefit on the PC's check. This might include offering a unique

positive modifier, reducing the Danger, or amplifying the rewards upon

success. It's essential that the GM communicates these benefits to the

player before they initiate the check.

Static checks do not allow you to invoke elements to enhance them.

Double Take

After being informed of both the Difficulty and Danger associated with

the Dynamic Skill check, players have three options:

  1. Proceed with the Dynamic Skill check.
  1. Opt for an alternative course of action.
  1. Abstain from action and wait to see what happens.

When performing checks outside of combat, players are encouraged to ask the GM about the Danger and Difficulty if they haven't been set. These are never secret, but it can be easy for the GM to forget to mention them when calling for a check.

Opposing Skill Checks

In response to an action, PCs and NPCs may oppose a Skill check.

Opposing checks are called for in the rules or by the GM when

appropriate.

When a PC opposes a check, they perform a Skill check with an

appropriate Skill to block their opponent's action. Each of the PC's

successes cancels out one of the opposition's successes.

  • If the PC's check cancels all of the opposition's successes, the

opposition's successful check becomes a failed one.

  • If the PC's check doesn't cancel all of the opposition's successes,

it can still prevent critical and supercritical successes by

dropping the opposition's number of successes below the threshold

required to achieve that degree of success.

NPCs do not roll to oppose PCs' checks. When an NPC opposes a check,

they apply a negative modifier (their opposing modifier) to the Skill

check. If an NPC does not have an opposing Skill modifier, they use the

Attribute modifier associated with that Skill.

A Bandit (NPC) tries to Grapple Fabrizio by performing a Might check.
Below is the Bandit's Might Rank. Because Fabrizio is a PC, he
opposes by performing his own Might check. The Bandit rolls 2d8+2d6
and gets two successes in his check, but so does Fabrizio. Fabrizio's
successes cancel the Bandit's. The Bandit thus fails at his attempt
to Grapple Fabrizio.
Fabrizio attempts to Grapple the bandit himself by performing a Might
check. Since the Bandit is an NPC, he opposes using his Might's
opposing modifier, the -2 in the parenthesis. A -2 modifier is
applied to Fabrizio's Might check. The Bandit does not make a roll
against Fabrizio's check.

*The bandit's opposing Might Modifier is in the parenthesis for that

skill.*

Assisting Skill Checks

When a creature wants to help another creature right next to them that

is making a Skill check, they can offer to perform the Assist action.

When Assisting a check, the Assisting creature evaluates their dice

pool as if they were making the same Skill check. They can add up to two

dice from their dice pool to the pool of the creature they are

Assisting.

  • A player should only offer to Assist a check if there is a

reasonable way for their PC to do so in-character.

  • Creatures cannot Assist checks that attack or use magic on other

creatures.

  • A PC can offer to Assist using a different Skill than the original

check, but must explain to the GM how that Skill would help.

  • The PC receiving the Assist must willingly accept it and can only

benefit from one Assist at a time.

  • If a Skill check has been assisted, it can't be pushed ([see page

9](#_Pushing_a_Skill)).

Group Skill Checks

When the entire group undertakes a shared activity or faces an event in

need of a collective roll, the GM can call for a Group Skill check.

To perform a group Skill check:

  • Each participating player makes an individual Skill check.
  • If half or more of the individual checks succeed, the Group check is

considered successful.

  • While players can use traits and abilities to boost their individual

checks, the consequences of failure only occur if the Group check

fails.

  • If someone raises or lowers the Danger or Pushes a check, it affects

the Danger for everyone involved.

In a Dynamic Group Skill check, a PC's invoked elements only affect the Difficulty and Danger Levels for their individual check, not the group's.

Skill Saves

Some effects, like Spells, traps, and explosions, have an instant

consequence. In these instances, creatures may be given the option to

make a Skill save to avoid the effect or reduce the damage. When a

creature makes a Skill save, they make the relevant Skill check when the

effect takes place. Skill Saves cannot be Assisted.

When making a Skill save to reduce damage, each degree of success

yields a different result:

  • Success: The creature only takes half of the damage dealt by the

effect.

  • Critical Success: The creature takes only a quarter of the

damage dealt by the effect.

  • Supercritical Success: The creature takes no damage from the

effect.

A Skill save against a single effect applies to each damage type and

amount; you do not have to save twice if an attack does two types of

damage. Effects that don't deal damage state what occurs when a creature

succeeds on a Skill save.

Fabrizio is standing near a barrel that's about to explode. He makes a Move save against an explosion that deals 12 Pierce damage and 10 Fire damage. He gets one success, so he halves both the Pierce and the Fire damage, instead taking 6 Pierce and 5 Fire damage.

Affecting Checks

Here is how to determine when an effect from an ability, Spell, or other

ruling would modify a creature's dice pool for a check:

  • Effects that impact a specific Attribute's check impact all

checks that would use that Attribute.

  • Effects that impact a specific Skill's check involve all checks

that would use that Skill.

  • Effects that impact an attack, Spell, or any specific action

only impact checks associated with that specific attack, Spell, or

action.

Pushing a Skill Check

When a PC fails a Skill check and has at least one Rank in either the

Attribute or Skill, or is benefiting from at least a +1 modifier, they

may Push that check by rerolling all the dice that did not land on

a 1. PCs may not Push a check if they are being Assisted or if the

negative modifiers would reduce their dice pool to 0. PCs can Push as

many checks as they like unless they have Pushed Beyond.

Any 1 rolled during a Push results in a Break. A Break makes the

pushed check fail unless it is a Critical or Supercritical success.

Pushing Beyond

PCs who Break can choose to Push Beyond, ignoring all Breaks. PCs

who Push Beyond cannot Push again until they finish an Intermission, a

five-minute recovery period. (More about Intermissions can be found on

page 14.)

  • PCs that Push a Skill check in in Tactical Combat ([page

15](#tactical-combat-1)) can't Push another until the start of their

next turn.

  • Pushing a Dynamic Skill check that has a Danger greater than

Safe will have you receive a second consequence from that Danger if

it fails or Breaks.

While PCs can Push any check they have failed, NPCs cannot Push any checks. Pushing a check is exclusive to PCs.

Status Points

Each creature generally has three types of Status Points to determine

their physical and mental state. These Status Points are as follows:

  • Health Points (HP) measure physical health and well-being.

  • Mental Points (MP) measure mental health and sanity.
  • Defense Points (DP) measure physical defense, armor, reflexes,

and natural fortitude.

A creature that has their current HP reduced to half their max HP or

less is considered Bloodied. Some actions have different effects on

a Bloodied character.

Temporary Status Points (Temp HP, Temp MP, and Temp DP) can be

gained through Spells and other effects and do not have a max value.

  • Temporary Status Points cannot be healed, repaired, or replenished.
  • Temporary Status Points from two or more different sources do not

stack. If you would gain Temporary Status Points and you already

have some, you either replace all current Temporary Status Points

with the new amount (if the amount you would gain is more than the

amount you currently have) or you do not gain the new Temporary

Status Points.

Taking Damage

Creatures can take two different types of damage, which reduce specific

Status Points.

Physical damage reduces DP first. If the damage reduces DP to 0, any

leftover damage carries over to HP. The physical damage types are

Blunt, Slash, Pierce, Frost, Fire, Electric, Acid, Necrotic, Radiant,

and Force. 

Mental damage ignores DP and directly reduces MP. The mental damage

types are Psychic and Fear.

Resistance, Vulnerability, and Immunity

Creatures can be more resistant or more vulnerable to certain types of

damage.

  • A creature's HP, MP, and DP each have their own separate

resistances, immunities, and vulnerabilities.

  • Creatures with resistance take half damage from that damage type

(minimum of 1) to the associated Status Points.

  • Creatures who are vulnerable take double the damage from that damage

type to the associated Status Points.

  • Creatures with immunity ignore all damage of that type to the

associated Status Points.

  • If a creature has both a vulnerability and a resistance to a

specific damage type, they take damage as normal. If they also have

immunity, they take no damage.

Other Damage Reductions

Some effects allow for a Skill save to reduce damage. Some abilities and

Spells reduce damage taken by a flat amount.

  • Flat damage reduction cannot reduce damage below 1.
  • If a creature takes more than one damage type at once, they choose

the order in which they receive them.

When a creature has multiple means of damage reduction, they apply them

in this order:

  1. Applying flat damage reduction
  1. Effects that halve or quarter damage
  1. Applying resistances and vulnerabilities

Fabrizio gets caught in an explosion that deals 6 Pierce and 6 Fire damage, but his HP is resistant to Fire damage. Fabrizio has 6 DP and chooses to absorb the Pierce damage first. The DP absorbs all the Pierce damage. The remaining 6 Fire damage is halved against his HP, rounded down. So, Fabrizio's HP only takes 3 Fire damage.

Sorcery and Spells

All creatures and arcane things in Realms of Legacy are made of

Sorce, the magical power that fuels the Veil. Creatures capable of

using Sorce have Sorce Points, which allow them to cast Spells and

perform abilities.

Casting a Spell with an Arcane Foci, an enchanted piece of gear that

Spellcasters wield to act as a conduit for their power, allows the

creature to make a Spell check. If the creature does not have an

Arcane Foci, they can still cast the Spell, but they cannot attempt to

enhance its power.

Every Spell hails from a specific Study of Sorcery. A creature's

Rank in that Study determines their proficiency in casting Spells from

that Study.

Spells are assigned a magnitude from Rank 0 to 5. Rank 0 Spells do not

cost any resources but require a check using the creature's

Spellcasting Attribute. A creature's Spellcasting Attribute can be

Wits or Empathy which is determined by their Class. Rank 1 and higher

Spells may allow for Spell checks. Spell checks are not rolled to see if

a creature succeeds at casting a Spell but to determine how powerful the

Spell is.

To perform a Spell check, create a dice pool based on the

Spellcasting Attribute's Rank and the highest Rank the creature has

obtained in the Spell's Study. Spells are expanded upon in Sorcery

(page 184).

Spell Check Degrees of Success:

  • No successes: Use the base Power Level of the Spell.
  • Success: Add 2 Power Levels to the Spell.
  • Critical Success: Add 6 Power Levels to the Spell.
  • Supercritical Success: Add 10 Power Levels to the Spell.
  • Some Spells have specific exceptions spelled out in their

descriptions.

Spell Check Step-by-Step

Check your Stats First, determine the Spellcasting Attribute Rank

and the Spell's Study (Talent) Rank.

  1. The Rank with the higher value is how many Base dice are added to

the dice pool.

  • Let's assume your Spellcasting Attribute is Wits. If you have a Rank

> 2 Wits and your Rank in that Spell's Study is 3, you start with 3

> Base dice.

  1. If the Ranks of your Attribute and Study are equal, then you can use

either Rank to determine how many Base dice your dice pool starts

with.

  1. The lower Rank determines how many Base dice are transformed into

Proficiency dice. If they are equal, all your dice become

Proficiency dice.

APPLY MODIFIER(S): Apply any positive and negative modifiers to the

check.

Impairment and Injuries

When a PC's HP or MP is reduced to 0, they gain the Impaired

Condition and roll for either a Wound or Trauma, respectively.

While Impaired, PCs can still move and take actions, but gain the

Shocked, Dazed, and Slowed condition which includes the following

penalties:

  • Reduce Speed by half.
  • Only allow one action per turn. Free Actions can still be used and

are not counted as your one action.

  • Prohibit Reactions.
  • Prohibit focusing on Spells.
  • Apply a -2 modifier on all Skill and Spell checks.

When NPCs are Impaired, they become Unconscious until they are no longer

Impaired. NPCs gain Doom Stacks instead of Wounds or Traumas. See

page 29 for more information on NPC Doom

Stacks.

Once their HP or MP have recovered at least 1 point, the PC is no longer

Impaired, and all additional Conditions associated with the Impairment

(along with their associated penalties) are removed.

Wounds

These are scars PCs receive from taking too much physical damage. Each

time a PC's HP is reduced to 0, or when a PC's HP takes damage while at

0, they gain a Wound.

To gain a Wound: Roll 1d6 plus an additional 1d6 for each Wound the

PC already has. Add up the results, then consult the Wound table. The

higher the roll, the more severe the Wound.

Traumas

These are mental scars creatures receive from taking too much mental

damage. Each time a creature's MP is reduced to 0, or when a creature's

MP takes damage while at 0, they gain a Trauma.

To gain a Trauma: Roll 1d6 plus an additional 1d6 for each Trauma

the PC already has. Add up the results, then consult the Trauma table.

Similar to Wounds, the higher the roll, the more severe the Trauma.

If you roll a Wound or Trauma that you already have, you gain the next highest Wound or Trauma listed on the table.

Critical Wounds/Traumas

A Critical Wound or Trauma is triggered when damage not only depletes a

PC's Status Points to 0, but the excess damage equals that Status

Point's maximum value. For a Critical Wound or Trauma, roll as for a

regular Wound or Trauma but with two additional Base dice.

Recovering from Impairment

Magic and Elixirs can replenish missing HP and MP, but when those

options are not available, creatures can perform the Bandage or

Galvanize actions on Impaired creatures. Bandage restores a

creature's HP and Galvanize restores a creature's MP. Each action

requires a Skill check.

Recovering Wounds/Traumas

Each Wound and Trauma has different recovery requirements.

  • Light Rest (LR): The creature must finish a Light or Full Rest.
  • Full Rest (FR): The creature must finish a Full Rest.
  • Permanent (P): The creature requires powerful magic or

prosthetics to heal this Wound or Trauma.

Healing Magic

Some magics can grant a creature HP or MP. Magic that restores HP or MP

to an Impaired creature resolves the Impairment but does not cure any

Wounds or Traumas. Some Spells can cure Wounds, regrow limbs, and even

bring a creature back from death (unless they have been

decapitated---only the most powerful magic can fix that).

Death

If a creature dies, their body is no longer considered a creature.

Instead, their corpse is considered an object.

Recovery Dice

Each PC has a pool of dice they can use to replenish HP, MP, and Sorce

Points. When a PC expends a Recovery die, they roll a d6 and regain HP,

MP, or Sorce Points equal to the result. They can choose which Status

gets replenished from the Recovery die before rolling. A PC has a

maximum amount of Recovery dice equal to their Level.

Intermissions

An Intermission is a 5-minute respite during which creatures can

perform quick, light activities like catching their breath, adjusting

their armor, and consuming meals.

  • A PC may take as many Intermissions as they want.
  • If an NPC or PC's DP comes from an armor set they are wearing, they

can replnish it completely during any Intermission.

  • If a creature does any strenuous activity during an Intermission,

such as attacking another creature, they do not get the benefits of

an Intermission.

  • After finishing an Intermission, a PC can expend any amount of

Recovery dice to recover HP, MP, or Sorce Points equal to the

result.

  • When an NPC finishes an Intermission, they can Refresh, regaining up

to a quarter of their max HP, MP, DP, and Sorce Points (if they have

any). Once an NPC has used Refresh twice, they cannot use it again

until they finish a Light or Full Rest.

Light Rest

A Light Rest is an extended period of downtime during which

creatures perform low-effort activities like sleeping, talking, eating,

bathing, and other non-combat and non-travel activities.

  • The GM must determine if the PCs are in a safe enough location to

perform a rest.

  • A Light Rest takes 7 days to complete. If any combat or strenuous

activity happens within those 7 days, the rest is interrupted and

must be performed again.

  • All Wounds and Traumas with a Recovery Time of Light Rest are

healed.

  • At the end of the Light Rest, a creature replenishes all their

Recovery dice. They can then expend any amount of their Recovery

dice to recover HP, MP, or Sorce Points.

  • A creature cannot benefit from another Light Rest until at least 1

day has passed since their last rest.

Full Rest

When creatures perform a Light Rest in a Safe Haven, a location that

provides plenty of resources (sleeping at a modest inn, staying as a

guest in a castle, or residing in any other friendly home), their Rest

is upgraded to a Full Rest.

  • The GM determines if the resting location is considered secure and

full of enough resources to be considered a Safe Haven.

  • All Wounds and Traumas with a Recovery Time of Light Rest and Full

Rest are healed.

  • All Diseases are cleansed.
  • All missing HP, MP, DP, Sorce Points, and Recovery dice are

restored.

  • Any armor, shields, weapons, or other items that have been broken

are repaired.

Game Activities

There are three types of activities that PCs can engage in: Tactical

Combat, Freeform Roleplay, and Veilstrider Conflict. The GM

determines which activity the PCs will engage in during the game. While

each activity has its own rules for engagement, they all derive from the

core resolution of making Skill or Spell checks.

Tactical Combat

When engaging in Tactical Combat, creatures engage in combat through

ordered turns. Gameplay emphasizes strategic use of their actions and

movement. For the purposes of tracking effects, which have varying

durations, each turn represents six seconds of time within the game.

Action Types

Standard: Strenuous but impactful actions such as a weapon attack,

searching for a hidden object, or casting a powerful Spell. Creatures

may only take Standard actions during their turn.

Quick: Actions that require less effort and are easier to perform,

such as reloading firearms or shoving a foe. Creatures may only take

Quick actions during their turn.

Minor: Mundane actions that require little to no effort, such as

drawing a sword or opening a door. Creatures may only take Minor actions

during their turn.

Free: Actions such as dropping a weapon or ceasing focus on a Spell.

These actions don't deplete any resources, but certain circumstances may

have to be present for them to be executed. Creatures may take Free

actions at any time, so long as the prerequisites are met.

Reaction: These actions occur in response to a specific trigger. A

creature cannot use a Reaction until its trigger happens, and they

cannot use more than one Reaction on the same trigger. When Reactions

are triggered, they can be used at any point in the round, even outside

of your turn.

Fabrizio is being attacked by a sword-wielding creature. He can use his Parry Reaction, which is triggered whenever he is the target of a melee attack.

To manage these actions during gameplay, PCs gain a pool of Action Points (AP) to spend on them.

Instead of AP, NPCs have two actions and a reaction available at the

During Tactical Combat, you may invoke elements for a Dynamic check

made through a custom action. However, elements that require an action

to be invoked require that you spend the proper amount of AP for it.

Because a custom action costs a Standard action to perform, you will

only have two AP and a reaction to invoke things if you haven't spent

them already.

Freeform Roleplay

This game activity is considered active whenever players are not in

Tactical Combat or Veilstrider Conflict. Freeform Roleplay

emphasizes the narrative collaboratively crafted by the players and the

GM. Players express their characters' actions and intentions and the GM

narrates the outcomes of these actions using Dynamic checks.

Invoking Bonuses

While in Freeform Roleplay, PCs can invoke multiple actions, Spells, or

other abilities that use an action. This is because Freeform Roleplay

does not cover a specific amount of time and allows a single check to

cover a course of actions, rather than just one. You can even invoke

more than one Standard action.

Should the invoked action or ability have a resource requirement, such

as expending a Specialty Resource or Sorce Points, the PC must fulfill

that requirement to invoke it. Since Dynamic checks focus on a course of

actions rather than one specific action, a PC does not need to worry

about any Action Point limit in their Dynamic check.

Fabrizio decides to Brawl through some guards blocking his path. He can invoke his Boar Style ability and his Crane Style ability since he does not need to worry about Action Points, but he must spend the Sorce Points for both if he does.

While most invocations are positive, the GM may raise the stakes in other aspects depending on the narrative. If the PC tries to invoke something that would make their check easier at the expense of being reckless, the GM may offer to have it grant a positive modifier but also raise the Danger.

Fabrizio is attempting to pick a lock before the guards arrive, a task

the GM has declared is Risky with a Difficulty of -2 (Hard). Fabrizio

requests to invoke his animal companion crab from the Animal Friendship

Talent to distract the guards. The GM will allow this and have it

cancel the Difficulty, but states that it will also raise the Danger to

Dangerous by putting Fabrizio's animal companion at risk.

Dynamic Spell Checks

In Freeform Roleplay, a PC can ask to use a Spell in a way that is not

clearly spelled out in the rules. The GM can allow a Dynamic Spell

check instead of a Dynamic Skill check if they think the proposed

Spell use makes sense. To do a Dynamic Spell check, the PC does

everything they would do for a normal Spell check, including using any

Sorce they need. But to figure out the result, the GM should treat the

Dynamic Spell check like a Dynamic Skill check, giving it a Difficulty

and Danger level that makes sense. Dynamic Spell checks can be pushed

and break like a skill check and only need one success to achieve their

desired results.

Fabrizio wants to use the Spell Boreal Binds to grab a Dragon and pull it into a room. Normally the Spell only Restrains a creature, but the GM allows the creative attempt with a Dynamic Spell check. The GM states that, due to the size of the Dragon, the Difficulty will be -4 (Extreme). A failed check means the Dragon breaks free and retaliates, making the Danger Risky.

Dynamic Group Checks

In a Dynamic Group Skill check, any traits or abilities invoked by a

character that would grant positive modifiers apply only to that

character, unless the GM specifies otherwise. However, if the invoked

trait or ability changes the Danger, then it affects the Danger for

everyone involved in the group check.

Social Encounters and Downtime

Another main aspect of Freeform Roleplay is social interaction. PCs

can attempt to Persuade, Deceive, and Sense the Motive of NPCs they

interact with socially. These actions use Static checks by default,

but the GM can make them Dynamic. NPCs cannot perform these actions

against PCs. The GM determines if an NPC believes or is influenced by

another NPC without the need for a Skill check.

Freeform Roleplay also gives PCs the opportunity to explore various

downtime pursuits during Rests.

The Roleplay Expanded (page 140) section provides

additional rules for facilitating roleplay, handling Dynamic checks,

social encounters, downtime pursuits, and more.

Veilstrider Conflict

Tactical Combat or Freeform Roleplay are used to deal with threats on

the party\'s ship, depending on how big and dangerous they are. When the

party fights enemy Veilstriders in ship-to-ship combat or faces off

against the huge Leviathans that live in the Veil, then Veilstrider

Conflict rules are used to settle the fight.

Veilstrider Turns

Veilstrider Conflict is a system that works in turns. At the start of

each round, all of the enemy ships or Leviathan-sized creatures take

their turn, and then the party\'s Veilstrider takes its turn. If there

are any NPC veilstrider allies, they would go last in the round.

Ship Tasks

Instead of actions, Ship Tasks are used to run the ship during the

players' turn. Each PC may command one Ship Task per turn, guiding the

crew and overseeing the ship's operations. In addition to these

individual Tasks, the party also has access to a pool of Shared Ship

Tasks equal to 5 minus the number of PCs (minimum of 0). Any PC may

command a Shared Ship Task during the party's turn in addition to their

own. All Ship Tasks refresh at the start of the PC Ship's turn.

Commanding a Ship Task requires a Skill check based on the action being

attempted. To use their own dice pool, a PC must be physically present

at the appropriate station. PCs at the same station may optionally

Assist the check. Movement between stations is unrestricted during a

round, but once a PC commands or Assists with a Ship Task, they cannot

move again until the start of the next round.

Crew Dice

If a PC wants to command a Task at a station they cannot move to, or if

they choose not to use their own dice pool, they may instead use the

Crew Dice Pool to command a Ship Task. This represents asking the crew

members for help. Every ship provides a Crew Dice Pool that can be used

to substitute a PC's own dice pool in Veilstrider Conflict. Using the

Crew Dice Pool prevents the PC from Pushing the check, though other PCs

at the correct station may still Assist. The Crew Dice Pool cannot be

used to Assist a PC's check.

Ship Stations

Ship Tasks are tied to four specific stations on the Veilstrider:

  • The Helm controls navigation and maneuvering of the ship.
  • The Main Deck handles scouting and communication between stations.
  • The Lower Deck operates ship weapons and manages medical care in the

sick bay.

  • The Hold maintains the ship's Core and stores cargo and livestock.
Defensive Maneuvers

During the enemy's turn, PCs may be able to react to certain enemy

actions through Defensive Maneuvers. Each Defensive Maneuver has defined

trigger conditions that must be met before it can be activated. Using a

Defensive Maneuver does not count as commanding a Ship Task for the

round.

At the start of the round, Fabrizio stays at the Helm to Assist his ally during an Evasive Maneuver. Despite their efforts, the Lower Deck has been damaged and needs immediate repairs. Since Fabrizio Assisted, he is locked at the Helm for the remainder of the round. Though he cannot relocate, Fabrizio may still use his Ship Task to coordinate repairs on the Lower Deck by using the Crew Dice Pool to make the Skill check.

Ship Resources

The party's Veilstrider has three main resources that must be tracked

during Veilstrider Conflict:

  • Hull Points (HP) are tracked individually for each station and

represent how much structural damage it can sustain.

  • Heat is generated when the Core is pushed beyond its limits and,

if not properly managed, can cause damage to the ship.

  • Crew is essential for operating a Veilstrider. If the ship has

less than half its maximum crew, all Skill checks suffer a -2

modifier. If a ship's crew is reduced to 0, all Skill checks suffer

a -4 modifier.

Movement and Distance

Veilstriders and other entities within the Veil move independently,

often at differing speeds and along unpredictable trajectories. Rather

than trying to precisely track chaotic positions, Distance Zones are

used to represent relative proximity.

There are four defined zones, measured relative to the party's

Veilstrider:

Far \<-\> Distant \<-\> Nearby \<-\> Adjacent

At the start of combat, the GM determines the starting zones for all

enemy ships and creatures. NPC movement between these zones must follow

this order. For example, moving from Adjacent to Distant requires

passing through Nearby first.

Ally Veilstriders stick onto one enemy at a time, moving with that enemy when the enemy moves. They are always considered Adjacent to that enemy, but may be targeted by other enemies in the same zone.

Travel and Exploration

When the PCs wish to venture into the wilds or travel to a new location,

the focus isn\'t on daily events but on significant occurrences during

travel. PCs choose a destination or direction and collaborate with the

GM to determine the duration of their journey.

The GM determines how long the journey will take by assigning 1-8

turns of travel, depending on the distance or the difficulty of the

journey.

  • Close Destinations (Short Journey): 1-2 turns of travel. These

trips represent journeys to nearby locations or brief treks.

  • Far Destinations (Moderate Journey): 3-5 turns of travel. These

trips involve longer distances or challenging terrain.

  • Very Far Destinations (Long Journey): 6-8 turns of travel. These

represent extended or arduous travel, possibly across multiple

regions.

Each turn of travel is an abstraction that represents a segment of

the journey, rather than a precise length of time or distance. The GM

can describe each turn as necessary, e.g., "This journey will take four

turns of travel, each representing about one week."

Encounters During Travel

Each turn of travel should include a notable encounter or event that

highlights the most important or interesting moments of the journey.

These encounters can involve a variety of elements, including:

  • Conflict with hostile creatures or environmental challenges.
  • Discovery of a hidden location or treasure.
  • Interaction with friendly or neutral NPCs.
  • Uncovering a clue or piece of lore important to the adventure.
Exploration vs Travel

If the PCs are exploring a locale without a clear destination in mind,

the GM does not predefine how many turns of travel will happen. Instead,

the PCs pick a direction and the GM decides how much ground the PCs

explore in each turn of travel. After each turn of travel, PCs can

change which direction they are exploring.

Survival in the Wilds

The PCs are assumed to be seasoned travelers who are able to find food

and water during their travels. No checks are required for basic

survival, as this game focuses on adventures, challenges, and encounters

rather than micromanaging supplies. If an event comes up that would

challenge the resources needed for basic survival, such as a sudden

storm or loss of rations, then the GM can use or add a turn of travel

with the encounter focused on finding shelter or gaining food.

Travel Through the Veil

These rules apply not only to traveling on foot but also to sailing on

Veilstriders across the Veil. It is assumed that enough livestock and

resources have been stockpiled to meet the food and water needs for the

expedition. When the GM determines how many turns of travel are required

across the Veil, they should follow these guidelines:

  • Close Destinations (1-2 turns of travel): Used for traversing

between areas that are one realm away.

  • Far Destinations (3-5 turns of travel): For traveling between

areas that are two to four realms away.

  • Very Far Destinations (6-8 turns of travel): For journeys to

areas more than four realms away.

The number of \"realms away\" indicates the number of orbits a realm must pass through to reach another. Being \"one realm away\" means there is no other realm between you and your destination; it is the next realm you will enter.

Resting During Travel

During the journey, PCs are free to take Intermissions as often as

they need to recover from minor setbacks. However, Light and Full

Rests cannot be taken while traveling between destinations or

exploring by default. PCs will need to either come across specific

locations that allow for such or expend a special resource, such as a

camp supply, to perform the Rest.

Senses

Every creature possesses innate methods of perceiving their

surroundings. If not explicitly specified, assume that a creature has

standard hearing and vision capabilities.

Vision: With normal vision, a creature can see other entities if

they are within a light source. However, discerning specific details

about distant objects or creatures might require a Scout check. The GM

determines the Difficulty of this check based on the distance and

clarity of the object or creature in question.

Obscured: Anything (creature or object) that is outside a creature's

visual range or not illuminated by a light source is considered Obscured

to that creature. This Condition denotes that the entity cannot be

visibly discerned.

  • Obscured creatures can attempt a Stealth action to gain the

Hidden Condition.

  • Creatures who have revealed their position cannot attempt to Stealth

in the same location they had previously hidden until they

successfully Hide in a different spot at least 10 feet away.

  • If an Obscured creature becomes visible to their adversary (e.g.,

comes into light), they automatically lose the Hidden status.

Hearing: The ability to detect or comprehend sounds depends on their

loudness and how far they are from the listener. The GM has the

discretion to decide if a creature can hear or understand a particular

sound.

Fabrizio ventures into a dark cavern. Unfortunately, he can't see in darkness. To navigate, he lights his lantern, which illuminates a 60-foot radius around him. As he moves forward, he hears some creatures lurking in the shadows. These creatures are 75 feet away from him, just outside the lantern's light. Fabrizio decides to take a shot with his ranged weapon. However, since he cannot see his targets, they have the Obscured Condition for this attack. Unless he moves closer, Fabrizio must apply a -2 modifier on his attack roll.

Special Senses

Creatures can have special senses that affect how they perceive their

surroundings. These do not inherently replace their normal senses.

Nightvision: This creature can see up to 60 feet in darkness. This

does not let them perceive color in the darkness.

Gloom Vision: This creature can see up to 60 feet in darkness and in

Shadow Gloom. This does not let them perceive color in the darkness.

Shadow Gloom is magical darkness that consumes all natural light.

Divine Light does not destroy it but does suppress it. Creatures with

Nightvision cannot see through Shadow Gloom.

Tremor Vision: This creature can see other creatures as long as they

and the other creatures are touching a surface up to 60 feet away. They

can perceive creatures that are Obscured by Invisibility or Cover.

Blind Vision: This creature can perceive other creatures up to 30

feet away without visibly seeing them, including creatures who are

Obscured by Invisibility or Cover. This also grants immunity from

effects that would harm the creature if they looked at them.

Omni Vision: This creature can perceive creatures up to 120 feet

away, regardless of whether that creature is Obscured by Invisibility or

Cover. Creatures with Omni Vision can also discern if a creature is not

in their true form. This also grants immunity from effects that would

harm the creature if they looked at them.

Measurement and Distance

Realms of Legacy uses imperial measurements for distance and length.

Most movement and distances are broken up into 5-foot segments.

Movement

Every creature has a Speed, representing the distance (in feet) they can

traverse on their turn. A creature may move up to their Speed in

segments, allowing them to move in short bursts or all at once, so long

as the total distance doesn't exceed their Speed. When a Spell, action,

or other effect would reduce or increase your Speed, it is added to or

subtracted from all of your Speeds.

Speed Types

Creatures require certain Speed types to fly, climb, and swim.

Fly Speed determines how far you can fly. If you are flying in the

air and your Fly Speed is reduced to 0, or you gain the Prone Condition,

you fall. If you lack a Fly Speed, you cannot fly. You can only drag

creatures that are smaller than you in the air when Grappling them or if

they are on you.

Climb Speed is required to scale surfaces easily. If you lack a

Climb Speed, every foot of climbing will cost 2 feet of Speed. If you

are climbing and your Climb Speed is reduced to 0, or you gain the Prone

Condition, you fall.

Swim Speed is required to swim. While swimming, you are immune to

the Prone Condition. If you lack a Swim Speed, every foot of movement in

water costs 2 feet of Speed, and you suffer a -2 modifier to Strength

and Agility checks while swimming. If your Swim Speed is reduced to 0,

you sink at a rate of 10 feet per turn.

Different Speeds do not stack. When you spend any type of Speed to move,

it depletes that amount from all of your Speed types. Difficult

surfaces cost 2 feet of Speed for every foot a character moves.

Fabrizio has magically gained wings that grant him a Fly Speed of 20 feet. Fabrizio's normal Speed is 30 feet. When Fabrizio moves, he can fly up to 20 feet but must land to use his remaining 10 feet. He can only move 10 feet on land after flying 20 feet, even though his land Speed is 30. If Fabrizio moves 20 feet on land instead, he cannot use the rest of his Speed to fly.

Jumping

When you wish to jump over a gap or up to reach a ledge, you must spend

your Speed and treat the jump as part of your movement. If you jump over

a gap but run out of Speed before reaching a ledge, you must make a Move

or Might save with a -2 modifier, falling on a failure.

Falling

Creatures fall at a rate of 60 feet each turn. A creature can fall up to

10 feet without any penalties. A creature falling beyond 10 feet takes 1

blunt damage per foot fallen. For every 30 feet fallen the creature also

gains a Wound or Doom Stack.

When a creature would be shoved off a ledge that would cause them to

take harm from a fall, they can make a Move or Might save to grab onto

the ledge (or something else) and halt the fall. They must have a Free

Hand or let go of something to do so. If they are shoved while holding

on and haven't climbed back up yet, they repeat the save with a -4

modifier.

Size

A creature's size is categorized as follows:

  • Tiny: Anything smaller than 2 feet tall. Similar to small pets

or even bugs.

  • Small: Creatures that are anywhere from 2--4 feet tall. Similar

to children.

  • Medium: Creatures that are around the size of a standard Human,

around 4--9 feet tall.

  • Large: Creatures that are anywhere from 10--14 feet tall.
  • Huge: Creatures that are anywhere from 15--25 feet tall.
  • Gargantuan: Creatures that are anywhere from 25--50 feet tall.

Using a Gridded Map

If your group is using a gridded map with squares or hexagons, each

square or hexagon should represent a 5-foot area that a Medium or

smaller creature takes up.

  • A Medium or smaller creature can occupy a space shared by one Tiny

creature.

  • Large creatures take up a 2x2 square area.
  • Huge creatures take up 3x3 to 5x5 squares.
  • Gargantuan creatures take up 6x6 to 10x10 squares.
Not Using a Map

If you choose not to use a gridded map to track creature positions, you

can manage the scene in your head. This approach requires strong

communication between players and the GM to keep everyone aligned on

positioning. Instead of tracking exact distances, players only need to

know if their character is "close enough" or "within range" for their

actions.

Physical Limitations

Pushing and Pulling: Your character can lift, push, and pull an

object with a number of pounds (lbs.) equal to their Strength Rank

multiplied by 50, plus an additional 25. If the weight exceeds that, the

GM can allow a Might check to determine if they can handle more weight.

Holding Breath and Suffocation: A creature can hold their breath for

up to 2 minutes. If they pass that time limit and are in an environment

that does not have breathable air, they die.

High and Long Jumps: Normally a creature can only jump about 5 feet

high or over 5 foot gaps. If the creature has enough space to move 10

feet in a straight line before jumping forward or up, this jump range is

doubled. Anything beyond these thresholds could be possible if the GM

allows a Might or Move check. A creature's movement must still be spent

for these jumps or a Might or Move check must be made with a -2

modifier.

Eat and Sleep: If a creature that needs food, water, or sleep goes

more than 48 hours without either, they gain the Exhausted Condition

until they replenish what they are missing.

Handling Inventory

Your character\'s Inventory is not based on strict weight calculations,

but on a limited number of Slots spread across three Inventories.

Equipping an item from one of these Inventories requires a free hand.

Each slot can only carry one item regardless of size and weight.

Pouch Inventory

The Pouch Inventory represents pouches, pockets, or other small

compartments on your person. These are used for storing tiny, easily

accessible items. Each PC has six Pouch Slots and can pull out or stow

away an item into a Pouch Slot as a Free action during your turn.

Equipment Inventory

The Equipment Inventory is divided into four categories: one Armor

slot, two Back Slots, two Belt Slots, and one Kit Slot. These represent

the gear you have equipped, sheathed, or otherwise carried directly on

your person.

  • Armor Slot: Only one armor set can be placed here, representing

what you\'re currently wearing. Donning or doffing armor takes 5

minutes.

  • Belt Slots: Used for items that are easily accessible. An item

can be equipped or unequipped in one of the Belt Slots as a Minor

Action.

  • Back Slots: Reserved for larger weapons, shields, or other

sizable items. An item can be equipped or unequipped in one of the

Back Slots as a Minor Action.

  • Kit Slot: Holds a specialized kit. Equipping or unequipping a

kit takes a Standard action on your turn.

While holding an item, you can drop it as a Free action at any point.

Backpack Inventory

The Backpack Inventory is used for items too large for your Pouch or

Belt Inventory. These Slots are only accessible if you have a backpack.

Different backpacks offer varying numbers of Slots. Accessing your

Backpack Inventory to retrieve or store items requires a Standard

action.

Item Weight

Items don\'t have precise weight measurements but are assigned a

weight tag that determines which Inventory Slots they can be placed

in:

  • Tiny: Items that are small and light enough to be placed in any

Inventory Slot.

  • Light: Items that can be stored in your Backpack, Belt, or Back

Slots.

  • Bulky: Large or heavy items that can only be placed in Back or

Backpack Slots.

  • Oversized: Items too large for any Inventory Slots and which

must be carried by hand.

Conditions

While traversing the realms, a variety of Conditions can change

creatures' circumstances by adding positive or negative effects.

Conditions also communicate the current state of a creature. A creature

can have multiple Conditions at once.

Bewitched: When this creature tries to willingly attack, cast

harmful Spells on, or direct any harmful effects at their Bewitcher,

they must make an Insight save with a -6 modifier. On a failed save,

they can't attempt to target the bewitcher with harm until the start of

their next turn. A successful save allows the harmful action to be used.

The Bewitcher gains a +2 modifier to any social checks against the

Bewitched creature.

Blinded: Scout checks that rely on sight fail. Any creature or

object a Blinded creature targets gains the Obscured Condition. Melee

and Ranged attacks against a Blinded creature gain a +1 modifier.

Bloodied: When a creature's current HP is equal to or less than half

their maximum, they become Bloodied. Some actions have different effects

when targeting Bloodied creatures.

Dazed: The creature becomes confused and dizzy, unable to fully form

complete thoughts or speak coherently. They cannot focus on Spells and

gain a -2 modifier to all Wits and Empathy checks.

Enraged: An Enraged creature cannot distinguish friend from foe. At

the start of its turn, it must spend its movement heading toward the

nearest creature before taking any other actions. If it comes within 5

feet of another creature, it stops and immediately uses a Quick Action

to make a melee attack with a --4 modifier. If the creature is wielding

a melee weapon, it must use the weapon for this attack. Otherwise, the

creature makes an Unarmed Strike for its melee attack. The creature may

activate relevant abilities with this attack. If an ally and an enemy

are equally close, roll a d6: on 4--6, the creature targets the enemy;

on 1--3, the creature targets the ally. After this movement and

potential attack, the creature may act normally for the rest of its

turn.

Exhausted: An Exhausted creature cannot regain HP or MP from any

sources outside of Recovery dice and Resting.

Grappled: A creature's Speed becomes 0 and they gain a -2 modifier

on attacks against creatures that aren't Grappling them.

Hidden: Enemies are unaware of a Hidden creature's location and

cannot attempt to attack or target them. Hidden creatures gain a +2

modifier on their next melee or ranged attack roll. After they attack,

cast a Spell, or are seen or heard, they are no longer Hidden.

Impaired: Affected creatures gain the Slowed, Dazed, and Shocked

Conditions. While Impaired, the creature cannot be immune to or remove

the Slowed, Dazed, or Shocked Conditions.

Invisible: A creature that is Invisible gets a +1 modifier to

attacks and Hide checks. They are considered Obscured to creatures who

can't see Invisible creatures.

Obscured: Obscured creatures inflict a -2 modifier on attacks

against them. Enemies cannot target them with Spells or abilities that

require sight of their target.

Poisoned: Receive only half of any healing from any source except

Recovery dice and Full Rests.

Prone: A creature lying on the ground. Creatures can only crawl

while Prone. Every 1 foot of crawling costs 2 feet of Speed. Melee and

Ranged attacks targeting the prone creature and are within 5 ft. gain a

+1 modifier. Ranged attacks targeting a Prone creature that are greater

than 5 feet away get a -2 modifier.

Restrained: A Restrained creature's Speed is 0. Their Move checks

and attacks suffer a -2 modifier. Attacks against them gain a +1

modifier.

Shocked: A Shocked creature's body trembles uncontrollably, making

it harder to move around. They gain a -2 modifier on all Strength and

Agility checks.

Slowed: A Slowed creature's Speed is reduced by half, can perform

only one action on their turn, and can't use Reactions. Free Actions do

not count against the one action a creature can use on their turn.

Unconscious: An Unconscious creature cannot perform any actions,

fails any Strength and Agility saves (except Endure), and is unaware of

what is happening around them. They will wake after 8 hours.

Diseases and Curses

Diseases are infections that creatures can contract. They range in

severity from a small negative effect to a deadly effect that will kill

its host if not cleansed in time. Diseases without a set duration last

for 1 month.

While powerful magic can cleanse a Disease, it is also possible to

recover from one naturally over time. After completing a Light Rest, a

diseased creature may attempt an Endure save to end the Disease early.

Alternatively, another creature can use a medical kit to make a Medical

check during that Rest in an attempt to cure the Disease. The GM may

assign a Difficulty to either check. On a failure, the same creature

cannot attempt to cure that Disease again for 7 days. A diseased

creature that completes a Full Rest is automatically cured.

\

Curses are magical afflictions that bind to a creature and cannot be

removed naturally. The GM should create an act that the creature can

accomplish to be free from the Curse based on the nature of its source.

Unlike Diseases, Curses don't spread from creature to creature, as the

nature of the Curse often determines its targets.

Cursed Diseases continue to produce the Disease indefinitely. These

are nearly impossible to remove as both the Disease and the Curse must

be cleansed within 1 minute of each other.

  • When using an effect that cleanses a Disease, the Curse will reapply

the Disease after 1 minute if it isn't removed.

  • If the Curse is removed, but the creature is still Diseased, the

Disease will reapply the Curse on the creature after 1 minute if it

is not cleansed.

NPCs

As PCs adventure and expand their influence and legacy in the realms,

they may encounter creatures who are impressed by them or aligned with

their goals. These creatures might offer their services and can be

recruited as Followers. Each Follower has their own NPC stat

block. A PC should not be able to recruit a Follower whose Challenge

Level exceeds half their own. Once recruited, Followers offer their

services, handling tasks like delivering messages, completing chores, or

procuring supplies. While they are loyal and helpful, Followers do not

join in combat situations unless elevated to the status of a

Companion.

NPC Companions

PCs can recruit one of their Followers as a Companion. Companions have

their own initiative, actions, Skills, Attributes, and Spells. They

accompany PCs in their adventures and participate in combat. If the

NPC's loyalty to the PCs is sufficiently high, the GM can permit a PC to

control the Companion's actions during combat. However, outside of

combat scenarios, the GM retains control over the Companion. The GM may

also reassume control of a Companion if their loyalty to the PC is

jeopardized or if circumstances demand it.

Companions are meant to complement a smaller group of PCs. The total

number of Companions a party can have is equal to 6 minus the number of

PCs in the party, with a minimum of 1 Companion.

NPC Action Limitations

When controlling an NPC, remember that they do not use Action Points

like PCs. Instead, NPCs have 2 actions and 1 Reaction per round. Only

one of their two actions may be used to perform a Standard Action.

While NPCs can perform most of the actions that PCs can, there are some

exceptions. Keep these in mind when running an NPC.

\

Dodge, Block, or Parry: By default, an NPC cannot use these actions.

An NPC that can perform these actions will have it stated in their stat

block. Humanoid NPCs that become companions can Block attacks if they

are wielding a shield or Parry melee attacks if they are wielding a

non-Heavy melee weapon. When they do, they inflict a negative modifier

on the attack equal to the shield's defense rating or their Opposed

Brawl.

Taunt: By default, an NPC cannot use this action. Some NPCs will

have a special trait that allows a variant of this action to be

performed.

Summoned Companions

Companions summoned through spells or magical effects do not count

against the maximum number of Companions a PC can recruit or control.

NPCs at 0 HP or 0 MP

Normally, when an NPC reaches 0 HP, they are slain. However, the GM

may choose to have them become Impaired instead of being killed. When an

NPC becomes Impaired, they become Unconscious and gain one Doom

Stack instead of a Wound.

  • Each Doom Stack imposes a -1 modifier to all Skill checks.
  • If an NPC accumulates five Doom Stacks, they are slain.
  • NPC Companions always gain Doom Stacks instead of dying when they

become Impaired from damage.

Like PCs, NPCs can have the Impaired Condition removed with a Bandage or

Galvanize action. However, Doom Stacks only reset to 0 when the NPC

completes a Light or Full Rest.

Summoned Companions are always destroyed if they become Impaired or take

even one Doom Stack.

Whenever an NPC would gain a Wound (e.g., from a trap or hazard), they

gain a Doom Stack instead, even if it does not make them Impaired.

When an NPC reaches 0 MP, they are not slain nor do they gain a Doom

Stack. Instead they fall Unconscious until they regain at least 1 MP.

Gaining a Trauma reduces their current MP by half its maximum amount,

but does not give a Doom Stack.

Golden Rules

Before you dive deeper into this book and learn all the rules, you must

always remember these 6 Golden Rules:

  1. When you have two conflicting rules, specific rules override general

rules.

  1. The effects of abilities and Spells do not stack with themselves. A

creature cannot be affected by the same Spell, ability, or effect

more than once at the same time.

  1. When dividing numbers, always round down to the nearest whole

number.

  1. When asked whether damage affects specific Status Points, if the

creature you're controlling has any DP left, or if the creature

you're controlling is Bloodied (at half their max HP), be honest.

Many abilities, attacks, and Features are affected by these details.

  1. The GM always has the final say on a ruling and may change rules to

make the game more enjoyable for everyone at the table.

  1. While the rules are the guidelines to play the game, don't expect

them to be a defense for arguably poor decisions. Always discuss

your character's intentions and plans with the GM to ensure clarity

and feasibility.
